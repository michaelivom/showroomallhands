4 · HubSpot Usage & Discipline

HubSpot is not admin. It is consultation memory, follow-up quality and coaching visibility.

Minimum notes

Needs, room situation, configuration, fabric/color, objections, willingness to decide, timeline of decision and next step.

Lead status

Every visit is classified: Hot lead, Warm lead or Cold lead.

Timing

Every consultation is documented before end of shift. First follow-up task is created immediately.

What I expect from everyone

1 · Own the leadEvery showroom visit has a clear owner in HubSpot.
2 · Document properlyNeeds, objections, decision timeline and next step are written down.
3 · Start the day in TasksHubSpot stays open on the desktop. Every morning starts with checking tasks, appointments and follow-ups.
4 · Work from HubSpotEmails and calls are done directly from HubSpot via the Outlook integration.
5 · Set the next actionNo lead leaves the system without a follow-up task or clear status.
6 · Finish same dayHubSpot is updated before end of shift, not days later.

5 · New Follow-up Playbook

The follow-up should feel like the natural next step after the showroom consultation: personal, useful and calm.

Advisory continuity

Reference the actual consultation and continue the customer’s decision process.

Decision support

Reduce uncertainty around configuration, fabric, size, delivery, value and price.

Consistent performance

Standardize timing, CRM logic and message structure — not the human relationship.

Operational rules

Lead status for every visit. First follow-up within 24h. One real personal detail in every message. WhatsApp only with permission. Advisor owns the relationship.

Quality rules

One clear next step. Visual support when relevant. Helpful, not needy. Address objections early. Review 5 follow-ups per advisor per month.

What to avoid

Over-automation, pushy urgency, channel misuse, generic templates, weak handovers and no measurement.

6 · Pricing Feedback

Goal: identify hidden pricing potential from the showroom reality. Where could we capture more value without hurting conversion?

Sofas with pricing upside

Which models or configurations feel underpriced based on demand, customer reactions or perceived value?

Bundles customers already want

Which add-ons, modules, cushions, covers or services are often asked for and could be bundled more confidently?

Fabrics / colors too low

Which fabrics, colors or material options create strong demand or premium perception but are offered too cheaply?

Where price resistance is lowest

In which customer situations do people accept higher prices most easily?

Discussion rule

Separate true affordability issues from underused value arguments. We are looking for concrete pricing opportunities, not a general debate about “too expensive”.
